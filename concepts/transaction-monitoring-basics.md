# Transaction Monitoring Basics

## Purpose

This note summarizes the basic concept of transaction monitoring for financial crime, fraud-risk, AML review, and risk operations.

## What Transaction Monitoring Means

Transaction monitoring is the process of reviewing financial activity to identify unusual, suspicious, or high-risk patterns.

The goal is to compare observed transactions with the expected customer profile, historical activity, and known risk indicators.

## Why Transaction Monitoring Matters

Transaction monitoring helps organizations:

- detect suspicious financial activity
- identify unusual transaction patterns
- review activity inconsistent with the customer profile
- reduce fraud and financial crime risk
- support AML and compliance obligations
- escalate cases that require deeper review

## Common Transaction Monitoring Triggers

A transaction monitoring alert may be triggered by:

- sudden increase in transaction volume
- multiple transfers within a short time period
- transactions to newly added beneficiaries
- activity involving high-risk jurisdictions
- transactions inconsistent with customer profile
- unusual cash or payment activity
- rapid movement of funds
- repeated failed or unusual account activity before transfers

## Key Analyst Questions

When reviewing a transaction monitoring case, an analyst may ask:

- What activity was observed?
- Is the activity expected for this customer?
- Has the customer shown this pattern before?
- Is there a new beneficiary or destination?
- Is the transaction amount unusual?
- Is the timing or frequency unusual?
- Are high-risk jurisdictions involved?
- Are there recent account changes or login events?
- Is additional information required?
- Should the case be escalated?

## Evidence Reviewed

A transaction monitoring review may include:

- transaction history
- customer profile
- expected account activity
- beneficiary information
- account change history
- login or device activity
- previous alerts or case notes
- source of funds information
- internal risk rating

## Risk Assessment

The analyst should compare the observed activity with the customer’s expected behavior and available evidence.

A transaction should not be treated as suspicious only because it is unusual. The analyst should review context, frequency, timing, customer profile, and supporting information before making a risk-based decision.

## Escalation Triggers

A case may require escalation when:

- the activity cannot be explained by the customer profile
- the transaction pattern appears structured or repeated
- funds appear to move quickly without clear purpose
- high-risk jurisdictions or beneficiaries are involved
- recent account takeover indicators are present
- there is adverse media or sanctions exposure
- the evidence suggests possible fraud or financial crime risk

## Example Analyst Wording

Potential transaction monitoring concern identified. The account showed multiple transfers within a short time period to a newly added beneficiary. The activity appears inconsistent with the customer’s previous transaction pattern and should be reviewed to determine whether it is expected, authorized, and consistent with the customer profile.
