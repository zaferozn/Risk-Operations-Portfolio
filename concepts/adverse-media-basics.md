# Adverse Media Basics

## Purpose

This note summarizes the basic concept of adverse media review for due diligence, compliance research, financial crime risk, and investigations-related work.

## What Adverse Media Means

Adverse media refers to negative public information about a person, company, organization, or related party.

This information may appear in news articles, regulatory notices, court records, public reports, or other open sources.

Adverse media does not automatically prove wrongdoing. It is a risk signal that requires careful review, verification, and documentation.

## Why Adverse Media Matters

Adverse media review helps organizations identify:

- reputational risk
- financial crime risk
- fraud indicators
- corruption or bribery concerns
- sanctions-related concerns
- regulatory issues
- links to high-risk individuals or entities
- inconsistencies between a stated profile and public information

## Common Adverse Media Topics

Adverse media may involve:

- fraud allegations
- financial misconduct
- corruption or bribery
- money laundering concerns
- sanctions exposure
- regulatory enforcement
- litigation
- organized crime links
- tax evasion allegations
- reputational concerns

## Key Review Questions

When reviewing adverse media, an analyst should ask:

- Does the source appear credible?
- Is the information recent or outdated?
- Does the article refer to the same person or company?
- Is the name match reliable?
- Is the finding based on allegation, investigation, charge, conviction, or regulatory action?
- Is the finding directly connected to the reviewed subject?
- Are there multiple independent sources?
- Does the finding require escalation?

## Source Credibility

Source credibility is important.

A credible source may include:

- established news organizations
- official regulatory notices
- court or government records
- public enforcement actions
- recognized industry or compliance sources

A weaker source may include:

- unclear websites
- copied content without attribution
- opinion-based posts
- outdated or unverifiable claims
- sources with no clear connection to the subject

## Entity Matching

Entity matching means confirming whether the adverse media refers to the same person or company under review.

Analysts may compare:

- full name
- date of birth
- country
- address
- company registration number
- directors or owners
- business activity
- related parties
- location and timeline

A name match alone is not enough to confirm that the finding belongs to the reviewed subject.

## Recency and Relevance

Not all adverse media has the same weight.

An analyst should consider:

- when the event occurred
- whether the issue is ongoing
- whether there was a final outcome
- whether the concern is directly relevant to the current review
- whether the issue affects the risk decision

Recent, credible, and directly connected findings usually carry more risk weight than old, unclear, or weakly connected information.

## Risk Assessment

Adverse media should be assessed in a neutral and evidence-based way.

The analyst should avoid assuming guilt or making unsupported conclusions. The assessment should focus on what the sources say, how reliable they appear, and whether the finding creates a relevant risk concern.

## Escalation Triggers

A case may require escalation when:

- the adverse media is credible and recent
- the finding is directly connected to the reviewed subject
- the media involves fraud, corruption, money laundering, sanctions, or regulatory action
- there are multiple independent sources
- the finding conflicts with the customer or company profile
- the case requires enhanced due diligence

## Example Analyst Wording

Potential adverse media concern identified. Open-source review found negative public information connected to alleged financial misconduct involving the reviewed entity. The finding requires further assessment to confirm source credibility, entity match, recency, and relevance before a final risk decision is made.
