# Due Diligence Basics

## Purpose

This note summarizes basic due diligence concepts for risk operations, compliance research, financial crime review, and investigations-related work.

## What Due Diligence Means

Due diligence is a structured review process used to understand a person, company, transaction, relationship, or activity before making a risk-based decision.

The goal is to identify relevant risks, review available evidence, and document findings in a clear and neutral way.

## Why Due Diligence Matters

Due diligence helps organizations:

- understand who they are dealing with
- identify legal, financial, reputational, or compliance risks
- detect possible links to fraud, sanctions, corruption, or financial crime
- make evidence-based decisions
- document the reasoning behind a risk decision
- escalate higher-risk cases when needed

## Common Due Diligence Areas

A due diligence review may include:

- identity or company verification
- ownership and beneficial ownership review
- sanctions exposure
- PEP exposure
- adverse media review
- fraud or financial crime indicators
- litigation or regulatory issues
- geographic or jurisdictional risk
- business activity review
- source review and documentation

## Evidence Sources

A due diligence analyst may review information from:

- public records
- company registries
- sanctions lists
- adverse media sources
- regulatory databases
- internal records
- customer-provided documents
- open-source information
- previous case notes or alerts

## Key Risk Indicators

Potential risk indicators may include:

- unclear ownership structure
- negative media related to fraud, corruption, or financial crime
- connections to sanctioned individuals or entities
- high-risk jurisdictions
- inconsistent customer or company information
- unusual transaction or business activity
- lack of clear source of funds
- complex structure without clear business purpose
- mismatch between stated profile and observed activity

## Analyst Questions

When reviewing a due diligence case, an analyst may ask:

- Who is the person or entity?
- What is the purpose of the relationship or activity?
- Is the available information consistent?
- Are there sanctions, PEP, or adverse media concerns?
- Is the ownership structure clear?
- Are there high-risk jurisdictions involved?
- Does the activity match the stated profile?
- Is additional information required?
- Should the case be escalated?

## Difference Between Due Diligence and Investigation

Due diligence is usually a structured review before or during a business relationship.

An investigation is usually a deeper review triggered by a specific concern, alert, allegation, or suspicious pattern.

The two areas are connected because both require evidence review, source evaluation, structured documentation, and risk-based judgment.
