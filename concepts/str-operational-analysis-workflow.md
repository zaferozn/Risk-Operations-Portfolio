# STR Operational Analysis Workflow

## Purpose

This note summarizes an operational analysis workflow used during suspicious transaction report review.

The focus is not only to understand what an STR is, but to practise how an analyst moves from an initial report to structured collection, source evaluation, hypothesis testing, link analysis, inference building, reporting, and dissemination.

This workflow is relevant to AML, financial intelligence, due diligence, investigations research, adverse media review, and risk operations.

## Analytical Workflow

An STR review can be treated as an intelligence-led analytical process.

The basic workflow is:

1. Examine the initial STR.
2. Identify the key persons, entities, accounts, transactions, locations, and dates.
3. Build an initial hypothesis.
4. Create a collection plan.
5. Search available information sources.
6. Evaluate the reliability and validity of collected information.
7. Collate findings in a structured way.
8. Identify links between persons, companies, accounts, assets, and transactions.
9. Analyse financial flows.
10. Develop premises and inferences.
11. Prepare written or visual reports.
12. Disseminate findings according to legal and institutional rules.
13. Use feedback to improve future analysis.

## 1. Initial STR Review

The first step is to examine the suspicious transaction report and extract the core information.

An STR may contain information about:

- who is involved
- what transaction or activity triggered the report
- when the activity occurred
- where the transaction took place
- why the transaction was considered suspicious
- how the transaction was carried out

At this stage, the analyst should avoid assuming guilt. The STR is a starting point for analysis, not proof of wrongdoing.

The initial review should identify:

- reported subject
- account numbers
- transaction dates
- transaction amounts
- reporting institution
- business or occupation
- stated grounds for suspicion
- related persons or entities
- previous reports or references
- missing information requiring follow-up

## 2. Hypothesis Building

A hypothesis is a possible explanation for the reported activity.

The analyst should test both legitimate and suspicious explanations.

Example:

Reported fact:

A customer owns a cash-intensive business and deposits large amounts of cash.

Possible legitimate hypothesis:

The cash may originate from normal business activity.

Possible suspicious hypothesis:

The cash may originate from an unknown or unlawful source and may be deposited through the business account to disguise its origin.

A hypothesis may change when new facts become available. For example, if cash deposits are made at different branches over a short period, or if the account was dormant before the activity, the initial hypothesis may need to be modified.

A good hypothesis should be:

- based on available facts
- testable through further collection
- open to revision
- separated from assumptions
- supported or weakened by evidence

## 3. Collection Plan

A collection plan identifies what information is needed to test the hypothesis.

The analyst should not search randomly. The collection plan keeps the review structured and helps track what has already been collected and what still needs to be requested.

A collection plan may include:

- information requested
- source to be checked
- request date
- responsible analyst or unit
- date received
- whether the information was recorded
- report or reference number
- comments
- reliability rating
- validity rating
- keywords or linked entities

Typical collection tasks may include:

- check STR details
- search FIU database records
- search name variations
- check account numbers and identity details
- review previous STRs
- check company records
- check domestic records
- review open-source information
- request foreign FIU information
- check financial flows
- review beneficial ownership
- identify linked persons, entities, and assets

## 4. Information Sources

Information sources may be grouped by access level and jurisdiction.

The main categories are:

- STR information
- FIU database information
- domestic closed sources
- domestic open sources
- international closed sources
- international open sources

The analyst should understand the difference between open and closed sources.

Open sources are generally available to the public, sometimes freely and sometimes for a fee.

Closed sources are restricted and normally available only to competent authorities, FIUs, law enforcement bodies, or authorized institutions.

## 5. STR and FIU Database Review

The STR itself is usually the first source of information.

It may provide:

- subject name
- account number
- transaction date
- bank branch or reporting institution
- transaction amount
- reported business activity
- stated suspicion
- narrative explanation
- related entities or accounts

The FIU database may then be searched for previous reports or related records.

The analyst should search not only the exact name but also possible name variations, aliases, alternative spellings, account numbers, identity numbers, company names, and related parties.

FIU database review can help answer:

- Has the subject appeared in previous STRs?
- Are there aliases or name variations?
- Are there related companies or accounts?
- Are there previous reports from other institutions?
- Is there a repeated pattern?
- Are there links to other cases?
- Is the activity new or already known?

Previous STRs do not prove suspicious activity by themselves, but repeated reports may increase the need for further analysis.

## 6. Domestic Closed Sources

Domestic closed sources are restricted sources within the analyst's own jurisdiction.

Depending on the legal framework, these may include:

- citizenship records
- address and property ownership records
- passport and visa records
- criminal records and ongoing investigation records
- vehicle registers
- company ownership records
- border crossing records
- import and export records
- administrative liability records
- tax or income-related information
- internet service provider and IP address records

These sources can help verify identity, residence, assets, movement, business activity, and possible links to other persons or entities.

Domestic closed sources are especially useful when the analyst needs official confirmation rather than open-source indications.

## 7. Domestic Open Sources

Domestic open sources are publicly accessible sources in the analyst's own jurisdiction.

Examples may include:

- company registries
- court records available to the public
- bankruptcy records
- real estate information
- local newspapers
- public business websites
- professional profiles
- public databases

Domestic open sources may help identify:

- company directors
- shareholders
- addresses
- business activity
- asset ownership
- public disputes
- insolvency issues
- media exposure
- local reputation indicators

However, open-source findings should be checked carefully. Open information may be outdated, incomplete, inaccurate, or require confirmation from official sources.

## 8. International Closed Sources

International closed sources are restricted sources from foreign jurisdictions or international cooperation channels.

These may include:

- information from foreign FIUs
- information from Interpol
- secure FIU-to-FIU communication channels
- foreign law enforcement channels
- foreign official databases available through cooperation

International closed sources may help when the case involves foreign companies, foreign bank accounts, cross-border transfers, foreign assets, or foreign criminal activity.

The analyst should respect legal restrictions, confidentiality rules, and dissemination conditions attached to foreign information.

## 9. International Open Sources

International open sources are publicly available sources from foreign jurisdictions.

Examples may include:

- foreign company registries
- real estate records
- court records
- bankruptcy records
- credit or corporate reports
- newspaper archives
- commercial databases
- watchlists
- sanctions lists
- social media profiles
- company websites
- domain registration records
- mapping tools
- image metadata

International open-source research can help identify foreign addresses, company connections, ownership structures, assets, public exposure, and lifestyle indicators.

Examples of useful open-source checks include:

- checking whether a person appears in public company records
- comparing a property value with reported income
- reviewing social media for links between persons
- checking image metadata for location or date information
- reviewing company websites and domain registration details
- checking whether related companies share directors, addresses, or contact details
- reviewing whether an entity appears on sanctions or watchlists

Open-source findings should be used carefully. They may support an inference, but they should not be treated as confirmed evidence without evaluation.

## 10. Source Evaluation

Collected information should be evaluated before it is used in analysis.

Two different questions matter:

### Reliability

Reliability concerns the source.

A source may be:

- completely reliable
- usually reliable
- fairly reliable
- not usually reliable
- unreliable
- impossible to judge

The analyst should ask:

- Has the source been accurate before?
- Is the source official or informal?
- Is the source consistent over time?
- Does the source have access to the information?
- Could the source be biased or mistaken?

### Validity

Validity concerns the information itself.

Information may be:

- confirmed
- probably true
- possibly true
- doubtful
- improbable
- impossible to judge

The analyst should ask:

- Is the information consistent with other facts?
- Is it supported by independent sources?
- Does it contradict known information?
- Is the information current?
- Is there enough context to interpret it correctly?

A reliable source can still provide incomplete information. An unreliable source can sometimes provide useful information. Therefore, source reliability and information validity should be assessed separately.

## 11. Collation

Collation means organizing collected information so it can be analysed properly.

The analyst may use a spreadsheet or structured table to record:

- information requested
- source
- request date
- date received
- report number
- comments
- reliability
- validity
- keywords
- linked entities

Collation helps the analyst avoid losing track of evidence, duplicated reports, missing information, and entity relationships.

Keywords can be used to tag persons, companies, accounts, assets, places, and reports. This allows the analyst to filter information by entity and quickly review all findings related to a specific subject.

Examples of useful keywords:

- subject name
- alias
- company name
- bank account
- address
- related person
- foreign entity
- property
- transaction route
- suspicious activity type

## 12. Association and Link Analysis

Association analysis identifies links between persons, companies, accounts, assets, and transactions.

Links may include:

- confirmed association between two entities
- suspected association between two entities
- confirmed membership in an organization
- suspected membership in an organization
- confirmed investment
- suspected investment
- directorship
- beneficial ownership
- shared address
- shared bank account
- money transfer relationship
- family relationship

A link chart or association matrix can help show complex relationships visually.

For example, the analyst may identify that:

- two persons are directors of the same company
- one company receives funds from another company
- a person controls or benefits from a company account
- a foreign entity is connected to a domestic account
- money is moved between companies and then transferred abroad
- different companies may be shell companies or part of the same network

The purpose is not to make the chart look complex. The purpose is to make relationships understandable and evidence-based.

## 13. Financial Flow Analysis

Financial flow analysis examines how money moves between accounts, persons, and entities.

The analyst should look for:

- source of funds
- destination of funds
- repeated cash deposits
- rapid movement of funds
- rounded amounts
- dormant accounts becoming active
- transfers through multiple accounts
- cross-border transfers
- payments inconsistent with the business profile
- possible layering
- possible use of shell companies
- possible structuring to avoid reporting thresholds

The analyst should ask:

- Where did the money come from?
- Where did it go?
- Who controlled the sending and receiving accounts?
- Were the transactions consistent with declared business activity?
- Were the amounts, timing, and frequency unusual?
- Did funds move through accounts without clear economic purpose?
- Were funds transferred to foreign jurisdictions?
- Were related parties involved?

Financial flows are stronger when connected with other evidence, such as company ownership, previous reports, travel records, property records, or foreign FIU responses.

## 14. Premises and Inferences

A premise is a factual statement based on collected information.

An inference is an analytical conclusion developed from several premises.

Example of premises:

- The subject made repeated rounded cash deposits.
- The account was dormant before the activity.
- Funds were transferred to a related company.
- The related company had weak evidence of legitimate business activity.
- The same individuals appeared across multiple companies.
- Foreign information linked the funds to another investigation.

Possible inference:

The activity may indicate that funds are being structured and layered through connected companies to disguise their origin.

Inferences should be conservative and evidence-based. Analysts should avoid overstating conclusions.

Useful wording:

- The activity may indicate...
- The available information suggests...
- The pattern is consistent with...
- Further review is required to determine...
- The evidence supports the hypothesis that...
- The information does not confirm wrongdoing by itself, but it increases the need for further review.

## 15. Reporting

The final report should present the facts, analysis, and judgment clearly.

A good report should include:

- summary of important facts
- subject and related entities
- transaction activity
- source information
- reliability and validity considerations
- link analysis
- financial flow analysis
- premises and inferences
- analyst judgment
- recommended next steps
- relevant supporting documents

The report should use clear and neutral language.

Avoid:

- emotional wording
- unsupported accusations
- unnecessary complexity
- passive and vague language
- speculation presented as fact

Prefer:

- factual wording
- active voice
- source-based statements
- cautious analytical conclusions
- clear separation between fact, hypothesis, inference, and judgment

Example wording:

The available information suggests that the subject may be connected to a network of related companies used to move funds across multiple accounts. The repeated cash deposits, rapid onward transfers, foreign links, and weak evidence of legitimate business activity increase the need for further review.

## 16. Visual Reporting

Visual reporting can help explain complex relationships.

Visual reports may include:

- link charts
- association matrices
- flow charts
- timeline charts
- transaction diagrams
- entity relationship diagrams

A visual report should make the case easier to understand. It should not replace written analysis.

Visuals are useful when the case involves:

- multiple persons
- multiple companies
- cross-border transfers
- layered ownership
- repeated transactions
- shell company indicators
- complex money flows

## 17. Dissemination and Feedback

Dissemination means sharing the report or intelligence product with the appropriate recipient.

The report should be tailored to the needs of the recipient.

Depending on the legal and institutional framework, recipients may include:

- internal FIU units
- law enforcement agencies
- prosecutors
- supervisory authorities
- foreign FIUs
- other competent authorities

Before dissemination, the analyst must consider:

- legal permission to share
- confidentiality restrictions
- judicial secrecy
- presumption of innocence
- source protection
- foreign FIU dissemination conditions
- whether the report meets the recipient's needs

Feedback is also important.

Feedback may show:

- whether the information was useful
- whether the analysis supported an investigation
- whether additional information is needed
- whether the report format should be improved
- whether trends or repeated patterns are emerging

Feedback helps improve future collection, analysis, and reporting.
