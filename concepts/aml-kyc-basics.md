# AML and KYC Basics

## Purpose

This note summarizes basic AML and KYC concepts for risk operations, financial crime review, due diligence, and case documentation practice.

## AML

AML means Anti-Money Laundering.

AML refers to controls and processes used to detect, prevent, and report financial crime risks, including money laundering, terrorist financing, fraud, sanctions exposure, and suspicious financial activity.

In a risk operations context, AML work often involves reviewing customer activity, identifying unusual patterns, documenting evidence, and escalating suspicious cases when required.

## KYC

KYC means Know Your Customer.

KYC is the process of identifying and verifying a customer and understanding their expected activity, profile, and risk level.

KYC helps organizations understand who their customers are, what type of activity is expected, and whether the customer presents increased risk.

## Why AML and KYC Matter

AML and KYC processes help organizations:

- identify suspicious activity
- understand customer behavior
- detect unusual transaction patterns
- reduce financial crime risk
- meet regulatory and compliance obligations
- support evidence-based decision making

## Common KYC Information

A basic KYC review may include:

- customer identity
- date of birth or registration details
- address or country of residence
- occupation or business activity
- expected account activity
- source of funds
- source of wealth
- beneficial ownership
- sanctions or PEP exposure
- adverse media findings

## Common AML Risk Indicators

Common AML or financial crime risk indicators may include:

- activity inconsistent with the customer profile
- sudden increase in transaction volume
- multiple transfers within a short time period
- transactions involving high-risk jurisdictions
- unclear source of funds
- unusual use of newly added beneficiaries
- repeated failed or unusual account access attempts
- adverse media connected to the customer or related parties
- sanctions or PEP-related exposure

## CDD and EDD

CDD means Customer Due Diligence.

CDD is the standard review process used to understand a customer’s identity, activity, purpose, and risk profile.

EDD means Enhanced Due Diligence.

EDD is a deeper review applied when a customer, transaction, jurisdiction, or relationship presents higher risk.

EDD may require more detailed evidence, additional source review, senior review, or escalation.

## Difference Between AML and KYC

KYC focuses on understanding and verifying the customer.

AML focuses on detecting and preventing financial crime risk.

In practice, the two are connected because customer information helps analysts decide whether activity is expected or suspicious.

## Example

If a customer normally makes small domestic payments but suddenly sends several large transfers to a new beneficiary in a higher-risk country, the activity may require review.

The analyst would compare the activity with the customer profile, review transaction history, check available evidence, and document whether the activity appears expected, unusual, or suspicious.

## Basic Analyst Questions

When reviewing a case, an analyst may ask:

- Who is the customer?
- What activity was observed?
- Is the activity expected for this customer?
- Is the transaction pattern unusual?
- Are there any sanctions, PEP, or adverse media concerns?
- Is the source of funds clear?
- Is more information required?
- Should the case be escalated?

## Case Documentation Focus

Good AML/KYC case documentation should be:

- factual
- neutral
- evidence-based
- clear
- concise
- structured
- escalation-ready
