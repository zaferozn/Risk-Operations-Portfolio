# Suspicious Transaction Reporting Basics

## Purpose

This note summarizes the basic concept of suspicious transaction reporting for AML, financial crime review, FIU-related analysis, and risk operations.

## What a Suspicious Transaction Report Means

A Suspicious Transaction Report, often shortened as STR, is a report submitted when a transaction or activity may indicate possible financial crime risk.

An STR does not prove wrongdoing. It is a starting point for analysis.

The purpose of an STR is to bring potentially suspicious activity to the attention of the appropriate compliance, FIU, or investigative authority for further review.

## Why STRs Matter

Suspicious transaction reporting helps organizations and authorities:

- detect possible financial crime risk
- identify unusual transaction patterns
- review activity inconsistent with customer profile
- connect separate reports or related cases
- support financial intelligence analysis
- escalate cases that require further review
- document risk-based concerns clearly

## STR Is Not Proof

An STR should not be treated as confirmed evidence of crime.

It is a risk signal that requires careful review, evidence collection, and analysis.

A transaction may appear unusual but still have a legitimate explanation. The analyst should avoid assuming guilt and should review the activity in a factual, neutral, and evidence-based way.

## Basic Analysis Questions

When reviewing an STR, an analyst may ask:

- Who is involved?
- What activity was reported?
- When did the activity occur?
- Where did the activity occur?
- Why was the activity reported as suspicious?
- How did the activity take place?
- Is the activity consistent with the customer profile?
- Are there previous STRs or related reports?
- What additional information is needed?
- Should the case be escalated?

## Information and Intelligence

Information refers to raw facts or reported details.

Intelligence is analysed information that has been interpreted and tailored to the needs of the end user.

Example:

Information:
A customer deposited a large amount of cash.

Intelligence:
The cash deposit may indicate potential structuring risk because it appears inconsistent with the customer profile and is similar to previous reported activity.

## Hypothesis Building

A hypothesis is a possible explanation for the reported activity.

An analyst may consider both legitimate and suspicious explanations.

Example:

Reported activity:
A cafe owner deposited a large amount of cash after the weekend.

Possible legitimate hypothesis:
The cash may come from normal weekend cafe sales.

Possible suspicious hypothesis:
The cash may be linked to structuring or laundering through a cash-intensive business.

The hypothesis should be tested against available evidence, customer profile, transaction history, and other relevant information.

## Collection Plan

A collection plan identifies what additional information is needed to test the hypothesis.

A collection plan may include reviewing:

- customer profile
- transaction history
- previous STRs
- FIU database records
- domestic records
- company or business records
- source of funds information
- related parties
- account activity before and after the reported transaction

## FIU Database Review

An FIU database may help analysts identify whether the same person, account, company, beneficiary, or pattern has appeared in previous reports.

Previous STRs do not prove wrongdoing by themselves, but they may indicate a repeated pattern that increases the need for further review.

## Domestic Sources

Domestic sources may include national or local records that help verify the subject, activity, or business profile.

Depending on the legal framework, these may include:

- company registry records
- tax-related records
- court or regulatory records
- property or asset records
- licensing records
- law enforcement information
- other official domestic records

## Risk Assessment

Risk assessment may consider both likelihood and consequence.

Likelihood asks:

How likely is it that the activity represents suspicious or high-risk behavior?

Consequence asks:

If the activity is suspicious, how serious would the impact be?

A risk assessment should be based on evidence, not assumptions.

## Key Risk Indicators

Possible risk indicators in STR analysis may include:

- activity inconsistent with customer profile
- repeated STRs involving similar behavior
- possible structuring
- unclear source of funds
- large or unusual cash deposits
- rapid movement of funds
- high-risk jurisdictions
- related-party concerns
- previous adverse media or sanctions exposure
- unexplained business activity

## Analyst Wording

An STR is not proof of wrongdoing. It is a starting point for analysis.

The reported activity should be reviewed against the customer profile and available evidence.

The hypothesis should be tested using transaction history, previous STRs, FIU database records, and relevant domestic sources.

Previous STRs may indicate a pattern, but they do not confirm suspicious activity by themselves.

The case may require escalation if the activity remains unexplained or inconsistent with the customer profile.
