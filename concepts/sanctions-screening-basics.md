# Sanctions Screening Basics

## Purpose

This note summarizes the basic concept of sanctions screening for risk operations, AML review, financial crime compliance, and case documentation practice.

## What Sanctions Screening Means

Sanctions screening is the process of checking customers, companies, transactions, and related parties against sanctions lists.

The purpose is to identify whether a person, entity, country, vessel, or organization may be subject to legal or regulatory restrictions.

Sanctions screening helps organizations avoid prohibited relationships, restricted transactions, and regulatory exposure.

## Why Sanctions Screening Matters

Sanctions screening helps organizations:

- identify possible sanctions exposure
- prevent restricted or prohibited activity
- reduce legal and regulatory risk
- support AML and financial crime controls
- detect high-risk relationships
- document screening decisions
- escalate possible true matches when needed

## What Can Be Screened

Sanctions screening may apply to:

- customers
- companies
- beneficial owners
- directors
- counterparties
- beneficiaries
- transactions
- countries
- vessels
- related parties

## Common Sanctions Alert Types

A sanctions screening alert may involve:

- exact name match
- partial name match
- similar spelling
- transliteration variation
- company name similarity
- country or location concern
- related party concern
- transaction involving a restricted jurisdiction

## True Match, Possible Match, and False Positive

## True Match

A true match means the reviewed person or entity appears to match the sanctions list entry based on name and supporting identifiers.

## Possible Match

A possible match means there is not enough information to confirm or reject the match. Further review or escalation may be required.

## False Positive

A false positive means the alert appeared risky at first, but after reviewing identifiers, the customer or entity does not match the sanctions list entry.

## Identifier Review

A name match alone is not enough to confirm a sanctions match.

Analysts should compare identifiers such as:

- full name
- date of birth
- country
- nationality
- address
- passport or ID information
- company registration number
- entity type
- ownership information
- related parties

## Key Analyst Questions

When reviewing a sanctions alert, an analyst may ask:

- Is the name an exact or partial match?
- Do the identifiers match?
- Is the country the same?
- Is the date of birth the same?
- Is the address the same?
- Is the entity type the same?
- Are there related parties involved?
- Is information missing or conflicting?
- Can the alert be closed as a false positive?
- Does the case require escalation?

## Common Risk Indicators

Potential sanctions risk indicators may include:

- matching name and identifiers
- incomplete customer information
- high-risk jurisdiction involvement
- ownership link to a sanctioned entity
- transaction involving a restricted country
- unclear beneficial ownership
- repeated alerts involving related parties
- conflict between customer information and screening data

## Risk Assessment

Sanctions alerts should be reviewed carefully and neutrally.

A similar name may generate an alert, but the analyst should not treat the alert as a confirmed match without supporting identifiers.

The analyst should document which identifiers match, which identifiers do not match, and whether the alert appears to be a true match, possible match, or false positive.

## Escalation Triggers

A case may require escalation when:

- key identifiers match the sanctions list entry
- identifier information is incomplete
- the alert cannot be confidently closed as false positive
- ownership or related party risk exists
- the transaction involves a restricted jurisdiction
- internal procedure requires senior review
- legal or regulatory exposure may exist

## Example Analyst Wording

A possible sanctions screening alert was identified. The customer name is similar to a sanctions list entry; however, the date of birth, country, and address do not match. A name match alone is not sufficient to confirm a sanctions match. Based on the reviewed identifiers, the alert appears to be a possible false positive unless additional information suggests otherwise.
